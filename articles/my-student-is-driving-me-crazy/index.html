My Student is Driving Me Crazy!!


If you are a teacher, most likely, along the way, there has been a student that has driven you crazy! Maybe you have multiple each year? This student pushes every one of your buttons! He/she is never absent, late or leaves early, and they never leave for a week trip to Disney! *For the sake of this article, let’s talk about, what schools refer to, as a “typical developing student”, instead of students that exhibit extreme violent behavior. I will address those experiences in another article.


Usually, teachers will react two different ways when dealing with this type of student. One, they may feel it would make them look bad to admit they have strong, negative feelings about a student, especially in the lower grades. It seems to be more socially acceptable to think that teenagers are supposed to drive you crazy!? Some may have the belief that good teachers should never dislike any of their students. Therefore, those feelings make us feel guilty!


Second, some may talk about this student, in ‘acceptable’ teacher terms like, ‘they are lacking in social skills, or they do not have any motivation or initiative (for a multiple of reasons we explore), or their parents did not teach them any manners,” etc. Whatever we use to justify our feelings of frustration, it is totally about them, not us!


The first reaction, is riddled with guilt that we are not good enough and the second reaction says our student is not good enough. In reality, we usually have a combo of both reactions. However, after many years of teaching, I began to question these thoughts. In other words, I began to wonder what my frustration about my student was really about. Was it that I was horrible person? Was this student really that horrible? Or was there something deeper going on? Was this more about me?

I concluded that all of the “accepted” justifications were really just distractions; distractions from looking deeper within myself! Even the guilt was a distraction. Guilt gets us nowhere; it just simply makes us feel bad about who we are and prevents us from moving on to what really matters; educating children at the highest level. I have never met a teacher, riddled with guilt of any kind, who was effective in the classroom. Guilt literally says, stop, you are not good enough to move on. You are not good enough to be forgiven. You are not good enough to gain wisdom from this experience. Guilt literally blocks our true self shining through and our students only get half of a teacher. The other half is busy telling him/her why they stink!


Next we look at the student. All of those reasons we came up with why they are so “bad” or wrong and causing a problem in our life. Many of those statements may be a fact about our student. But, I always felt like facts were unemotional! So, why did these facts stir up negative feelings? Well, the truth is, it is easier to see in others what we do not like in ourselves. Sometimes, our student is reflecting back to us something we do not like about ourselves. An example: Our student is quick to judge? Am I quick to judge? Other times, a student can remind us (unconsciously at first) of someone that hurt us deeply. Are these emotions that rose to the surface, really a part of ourselves that have not been healed?

It seems, those students that drive us crazy, represent something deeper about us! They are teaching us about ourselves. If we examine more closely the emotions they stir in us, we realize we have been given an amazing opportunity to learn more about ourselves. Almost as soon as we see the mirror, we begin to see that student in a different light; as OUR teacher!


Book suggestion: Loving What Is by Byron Katie
